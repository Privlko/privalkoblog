Building on the previous post, I want to look at group differences in these trends. We saw how incomes rose quickly since the 1990’s but that this growth declined in later years, possibly due to recession, maturity, or sanctions. However, these trend lines take the average rates for a given year and do not consider the change in inequality between groups over time. In this post, I want to focus more specifically on income, with the idea that I will focus on wages in a future post.

Average income trends in quintiles

One way to consider this growth, is to look at quintile differences in real income trends over time, comparing the five groups of average ordered income for each year. This is shown in the plot below.

The differences between categories sometimes widen and sometimes narrow, but generally the results show that income increased for everyone drastically. Specifically it is difficult to comment on inequality within years because of the log scale. Instead, we will consider two measures of inequality statistics within years; the p90:p10 ratio and the top decile’s share of total income for each year.

Income differences between percentiles as they are

Before considering these, we will briefly show the average earnings of each percentile for 2020 which are shown in 2020 rubles. This is shown in the figure below. The 3rd percentile, on average, earns just 10,221 rubles per month. We know from recent updates and policy reviews that the highest possible unemployment benefit was raised from 8,000 Russian rubles to 12,130 Russian rubles, so some of these respondents are likely earning unemployment benefit at most. Further, the minimum possible unemployment benefit was increased to 4,500 Russian rubles. This is in line with the findings presented here, the lowest paid individuals are likely receiving income from benefits and support like unemployment. Given that the highest possible payment is higher than the amount that we consider here, this suggests we are capturing those on unemployment benefits well. Further, we note that the 10th percentile earns 15,000 rubles a month according to the data, which is useful for calculating the p90/p10 ratio. Regarding top earners, we see that average earnings in the 97th percentile are 90,000 rubles per month, while the 90th percentile earns 60,000 rubles per month. Once again, these earnings are high and constitute a p90/p10 ratio of 4, which is above many European countries. For some perspective the United States has a p90/p10 estimate of 6.3 while Japan has an estimate of 6.1; at the same time, Denmark (3.0), France(3.6), and Germany (3.6) have significantly lower estimates between these percentiles. The estimate presented here brings Russia closer to Canada (4.0) and Australia (4.3) in terms of income inequality, which is closer aligned to many liberal market economies, than more egalitarian states like Finland, with whom Russia shares a border. A summary chart is listed below.

We further explore this ratio later, for the moment it is worth noting the high rates of inequality between percentiles in 2020 data. We also want to consider how these figures looked in 2012, when Russia had just began to recover from its financial crisis, partially caused by the wider European Debt Crisis.

We find that earnings were quite different in 2012, with far lower incomes in 2020 prices and seemingly fewer differences between groups. Specifically, we see that earners in the 3rd percentile have an average income of 3,049 rubles per month, which is far lower than the 3rd percentile in 2020 (roughly 29% of what is on offer in 2020). We also see that people in the 10th percentile earned 4,450 rubles per month in 2020 figures, which is again, roughly 66% of the same money in 2020. Regarding top earners, we see that those in the 97th percentile on average earned 36,586 per month in 2020 currency, while those in the 90th percentile earned 24,391 per month in 2020 currency. This suggests a p90/p10 ratio of 5.5 for 2012.

Before considering measures of inequality, we can also consider the changes in real income for each percentile. In order to compare change over time, we can consider changes for percentiles between two time points. Given the extreme volatility of the Russian economy in the 1990’s and the subsequent shocks in the economy, it worth considering time point both overall and separately. The graph below looks at the rate of change of each percentile for several time points (for example 2020 versus 2012). The first graph considers four sets of time points; 2020 vs 2012, 2012 vs 2007, 2007 vs 2000, and 2000 vs 1995. The y-axis compares the size of the change between the two time points. For example, for the 3rd percentile, income grew almost 25 times greater between 2000 and 2007; for the 97th percentile income grew almost 10 times greater during the same period. The most obvious difference in the plot is the gap in growth between 1995-2007 and 2007-2020.

For this reason, we split the two time points into separate graphs. Starting with 1995-2007, we first note the high levels of growth for each percentile over both periods, with incomes growing rapidly by a factors of 6-25 times greater. However, the period 2000-2007 was most beneficial to lower earning percentiles (whose income grew 24-15 times greater) and less beneficial to higher earning percentiles (whose income grew 8-12 times greater). For the median earner (50th percentile) incomes grew by a factor of 15 in the seven year period. Regarding the period 1995-2000 we see few differences between percentile groups over the period, with all percentiles seeing a 15 or 18 factor increase in incomes during this time.

If we focus only on more recent years, like 2007-2020, we see a significant drop in the growth of income (most likely due to recessions and other limits on growth like sanctions). However, here too, incomes are increasing by multiple factors in a very short space of time, and this benefits mostly those at the lower end of the income distribution, with lower percentiles seeing almost a four factor increase in incomes between 2007 and 2012, and between 2012 and 2020. Those at the higher end of the distribution are seeing a three fold increase in income from 2007 to 2012 and a 2.5 factor increase between 2012 and 2020, according to these estimates.

Percentile Ratios

The results of each graph suggest that growth and development has benefited those on lower incomes more than it has benefited those on higher incomes. In short this suggests that income inequality has decreased since the 1990’s. We can check this using the p90:p10 ratio mentioned earlier, which tracks the ratio between the 90th percentile income and the 10th percentile income. We can also build on this, adding additional ratios like the p50:p10, and the p90:p50. This is shown in the graph below.

Here we see a gradual fall in inequality from 1996, with the 1990’s capturing extreme differences of 10:1, the 2000’s capturing lower levels, and the 2010’s capturing a range of 6:1 and eventually 4:1. However, this is only relevant for the extreme groups of the p90:p10 ratio. The other ratios are more muted and typically hover around the ratio of 3:1, although here too there has been a decline in inequality.

In general, Russia’s p90:p10 ratio is on par with the p90:p10 ratio captured by the OECD for 2019, which has a ratio of 4.6:1. Further, the same statistics record a p50:p10 ratio of 2.2 for Russia, where we record a measure of 2 for the same year.

Top decile share of income aan other decile shares

Finally, we will consider the share of the top decile among Russia’s total income. As before this measure is tracked from 1994. This is listed below. The Figure shows that the top decile’s share of total income has fallen slightly, but that the change has been less extreme than the percentile share differences recorded above.

Finally, we can consider share of total income given to all deciles since 1994. We see that the top decile’s share of total income has gradually declined as other deciles have grown. However, despite this, the top decile also has the largest share of total Russian income.

In general we see that the lower income groups have benefited from economic growth and development, but that Russia still has high levels of inequality.
