Poverty, shoes, and Rosstat

2019/04/08

Rosstat, Russia’s main authority on state figures, recently released a summary of poverty and deprivation. One of the main findings suggests that 33% of households cannot afford two pairs of seasonally appropriate shoes for everyone in the home. A few publications picked up on the finding, and the Kremlin supposedly rejects the body’s figures..

Generally, Russia’s wages are famously low the median hourly wage works out at roughly 0.91 net per hour in British pounds. Compared to the British median of 5.93 per hour. Further, there is wide inequality and dispersion in earnings, which likely impacts households income further. On top of these points, opposition politicians have started pointing out that earners (especially those in the public sector) typically receive less pay than they are contractually obligated to receive.

However, the stats presented by Rosstat are particularly strange because they capture other measures of deprivation, which suggest that homes are comfortable with larger expenses but less comfortable paying for shoes. I translate and illustrate these below.

A brief graph summarises the results for all households together.

Over half of all households could not replace furniture or pay for a sudden cost like a medical procedure. A significant majority could not take a week’s holiday at a second home or with friends or family. The cost of new shoes registers as a significant issue for 33% of households, even though a holiday break at a dacha would be relatively inexpensive but may consist of taking time off work. Furtherseasonal clothing for the family only registers as an issue for roughly 10% of households, although this cost is likely comparable to shoes.

What’s strangest is that one in five households claim to not be able to buy fruit at any time of the year, but one in ten claim to not be able to buy pharamceuticals, which are obviously more expensive than meat, fruit or fish.

In general the data from Rosstat seems strange and at times, counter intuitive. One issue may lie with the wording of the question. The deprivation question about clothes focuses on a single family member, the question about shoes focuses on each member of the family. This is a stretch, but could impact theway questions are answered.

It’s worth briefly looking at the shoe question spreads across urban and rural Russia.
